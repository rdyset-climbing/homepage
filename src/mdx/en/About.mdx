### Showcasing Japanese climbing hold makers to an international audience.

Japanese climbers have earned their well-deserved reputation by achieving impressing results at the highest level of climbing, both indoors and outdoors.

<Image
  brandId="ubh"
  set="maguro_dt"

  caption="Under Blue Hold's famous “tuna” hold"

And Japanese gyms such as B-Pump or Next Gen are increasingly becoming climbing
destinations in their own right, often being featured on YouTube or social
media.

And yet, despite this increased awareness of the Japanese climbing scene, Japanese climbing hold makers remain relatively unknown abroad.

But as it turns out, Japanese makers are worth paying attention to as well.

<Image
  brandId="k16"
  set="surf_reserve_2"
  photo="surf_reserve_2_1.webp"
  caption="K16 offers unique no-texture macros"
/>
Despite its vibrant climbing scene, Japan has relatively few large-scale
climbing gyms compared to Europe or North America, due to real-estate
constraints. This is why Japanese setters have perfected the art of making the
most of limited wall space, ensuring every move of a climb presents a challenge
no matter the grade.

In order to support this setting style, Japanese shapers put just as much thought and effort in their work, crafting each new hold to target different finger and body positions, from tiny plastic crimps to huge fiberglass macros.

<Image
  brandId="mojo_holds"

What's more, many of these brands are individual operations, passion projects
started by climbers who end up splitting their time between the workshop, the
gym, and the crag–often in addition to their day job.

Ever since I've stumbled upon these makers, I've been fascinated by their work and their limitless ability to surprise with new hold shapes–all this often at very reasonable prices.

This is why ReadySet's goal is to give this hidden industry a little more exposure, and maybe even bring it a few new customers!
